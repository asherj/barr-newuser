Bay Area Rep Rap Presents: All Things 3D Printing

We created this document to help the folks who come to the meetings, who just don’t know where to start.  There’s a lot of info available, but it’s not easily digestible.   So hopefully these pages may serve as a starting place on your journey.  This guide is geared toward the new 3d printer owner.  If you are interested in building one, come to the meetings, there are lots of folks there with experience, happy to share. In sections where there are large numbers of items, we have tried to limit the choices to just a few that are more approachable.  The rest will be in the Resources page.   If you are more interested in 3D design, and would rather have someone else print things, ask around or look at the Service Bureau section.

Bay Area Rep Rap members have been sharing [ideas, tips, best term for concept here please] at local meetings since the earliest days of “repetative rapid prototyping,” aka 3D printing. The group has people who build their own 3D printers to those who are just starting on a creative journey. Just like you, all of us had to start knowing nothing; learning by looking for answers to questions. Answers? The web is strewn with videos, tips and tutorials, so much information is overwhelming, especially when you want a simple answer to what you thought was a simple question. For now, this guide will point you to our favorite answers to common questions we hear, to be replaced with more and more answers coming from our members as we build our knowledge base. Take a look at our Bay Area Rep Rap Discussion Group and feel free to post your quesiions. We meet in San Jose twice monthly – please drop by if you can… even bring your printer!

  • Tweak it for your purposes (TinkerCAD)
  • Convert it to g-code (Slic3r)
  • Send it to the printer (SD card or Pronterface)
  • Print

Sources of Models

There are a few different "types" of models, Some are intended as assets for games, or just for illustrative purposes. Some are scans of existing objects. Some are design models used in manufacturing. Models that are suitable for 3D printing need to have certain characteristics:

  • They need to be in one piece (no floating parts)
  • They need to be watertight (have a defined inside & outside)
  • The walls of the model must not be too thin (depends on your printer, but more than than .2 mm is a good start)
  • For FDM printers unsupported overhangs shouldn't be more than 45 degrees

There is more info on what makes a good 3D model in the Design section.
